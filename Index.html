Restaurant Liquidation Risk Modelling

25,268 businesses · Companies House · Google Places API · ONS IMD 2019

Key findings
  • 64.2% of liquidated businesses had overdue accounts filings vs just 6.5% of active ones — a 10× gap and the single strongest predictor in the dataset
  • Liquidated businesses are on average 15 months older than active ones (68.6 vs 53.5 months) — failure risk peaks in the 3–7 year window, not at launch
  • Pubs & bars liquidate at 9.9% — nearly double the sector average of ~5% and more than twice the rate of takeaways at 4.3%
  • All 7 BART sentiment dimensions showed <0.05 separation between classes — customer perception signals do not reliably predict business failure; financial compliance signals dominate
  • ONS IMD 2019 deprivation decile joined at postcode level confirms businesses in more deprived neighbourhoods face elevated liquidation risk independent of review scores
  • SHAP values rank filing overdue status, company age, and hygiene scores as top predictors — sentiment dimensions rank 11th–17th, validating the EDA findings quantitatively
Tools used
  • pandas · numpy · scikit-learn · imbalanced-learn (SMOTE) · shap
  • XGBoost · TensorFlow / Keras · Hugging Face Transformers (bart-large-mnli)
  • Google Places API · Companies House · FSA Hygiene Ratings · ONS IMD 2019

EDA Visualisation Criminal Justice

UK Crime & Justice Analytics

Ethnicity · stop & search · prosecutions · convictions · 2006–2023

Key findings
  • Black individuals stopped at 4–9.5× the rate of White individuals per 1,000 population — disproportionality peaked at 9.6× in 2017 and worsened after the 2014 reform even as total S&S volumes fell
  • Total prosecutions fell 2009–2017, yet conviction rates rose from ~76% to ~83% — fewer cases brought, but those that were brought succeeded more often, consistent with CPS charging reforms
  • Drug & public order offences yield >90% conviction rates once charged; sexual offences sit at ~52% — the widest offence-type gap in the data
  • Males account for 6.2× more prosecutions than females — larger than any ethnic gap in the dataset, and consistent across all ethnic groups
  • S&S of Black individuals generates prosecutions most efficiently despite the highest stop rate — raising questions about targeting thresholds and differential prosecution standards across ethnicities
  • Three derived metrics introduced: conviction rate, stop-to-prosecution efficiency ratio, and Black:White disproportionality ratio — none present in the raw government datasets
Tools used
  • pandas · numpy · matplotlib · seaborn
  • Three open government datasets from data.gov.uk · 2006–2023
